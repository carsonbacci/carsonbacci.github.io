Constitutive Modeling (VUMAT)

Thermoelastic Materials Simulations | Nov - Dec 2025

Motivation

To accurately simulate the complex, coupled thermo-mechanical responses of engineering materials undergoing finite, large-scale deformations—a capability that is absolutely critical for modeling high-speed impacts, metal forming, and extreme structural stress events.

Technical Approach

Implemented a custom Fortran VUMAT (Vectorized User Material) subroutine within Abaqus/Explicit. The constitutive model utilized multiplicative kinematics and Hencky strain measures to handle finite deformations, integrating adiabatic heating calculations, entropy balance, and isotropic representation theory to respect strict thermodynamic restrictions.

Outcomes

Developed a robust, nonlinear thermoelastic finite-strain material model capable of capturing adiabatic temperature spikes during large deformations. The numerical model was validated against analytical solutions across multiple materials, ensuring high-fidelity thermal predictions and computational stability during mesh refinement.

Solar Panel Thermal Simulations

Physics-Informed Neural Networks | ME 249 Projects

Motivation

To accurately predict photovoltaic (PV) power output and model 2D temperature fields across materials under varying environmental conditions (such as extreme irradiance up to 1850 W/m²). The goal was to establish a predictive framework that overcomes the high computational costs of traditional numerical simulations while simultaneously avoiding the overfitting risks inherent to standard, physics-blind neural networks.

Technical Approach

Developed and rigorously compared multiple machine learning architectures. Initially contrasted standard feedforward networks trained via gradient descent (backpropagation) against genetic algorithms to evaluate convergence behavior. Progressed to constructing Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) to approximate 2D temperature fields by embedding fundamental heat transfer partial differential equations and spatial boundary conditions directly into the network's loss function. Furthermore, the complex PV input datasets were normalized using median scaling, and one-hot encoded classifiers were implemented to predict the specific operating modes that would yield maximum power.

Outcomes

The optimized PINN architecture achieved a ten-fold reduction in model loss (dropping from 0.0034 to 0.000220) and converged in just 25% of the original training time (20,000 epochs), producing a physically consistent 2D temperature profile that closely matched reference analytical solutions. Additionally, the comparative algorithmic studies demonstrated that while backpropagation significantly outperformed the genetic algorithm in both runtime and precision, carefully tuning and balancing network depth and width was absolutely critical to generalizing PV power predictions on unseen validation data.

Remote Heat Sink System

Golf Club Aerodynamics

Wind Tunnel Modifying and Testing | Feb - May 2025

Motivation

To quantify how the aerodynamic engineering of golf clubs has evolved to maximize swing speeds and player performance. The experiment aimed to physically validate whether the modern geometry of the Titleist Tsr3 (2023) offered a measurable reduction in aerodynamic drag compared to the older Titleist 917 D3 (2016).

Technical Approach

1:2 scale 3D-printed models of both driver heads were fabricated and placed inside a wind tunnel. A strain gage sensor was oriented specifically to isolate and measure drag forces. Pressure transducers and Pitot tubes were deployed to measure tunnel pressure, and fundamental fluid mechanics theory (Bernoulli’s equation) was applied to calculate the corresponding air velocities and Reynolds numbers.

Outcomes

The experimental data definitively corroborated the hypothesis: the newer Tsr3 driver head was significantly more aerodynamic. By computing and mapping the drag coefficients against varying wind speeds, the analysis proved that the advanced geometric shaping of modern clubs successfully minimizes drag forces.

Blunt Nose Missile CFD Tests

Viscous Compressible Shock Analysis | Jan - May 2025

Motivation

Understanding high-speed shock formation and compressible flow separation over blunt nose geometries is critical for predicting aerodynamic heating and pressure distributions on supersonic aerospace vehicles and missiles.

Technical Approach

Viscous compressible flow simulations were executed in Ansys Fluent utilizing density-based solvers and the Sutherland Viscosity law for air. The system was tested at free-stream Mach numbers of 2.0, 2.5, and 3.0. A custom function evaluating the static pressure gradient was implemented to trigger automatic, iterative mesh refinement, ensuring high-resolution data capture precisely at the shock boundary.

Outcomes

Achieved highly resolved normal bow shocks, validating the numerical outputs against analytical normal shock relations with exceptional accuracy. The project successfully mapped 2D contours of pressure, density, and temperature, demonstrating the precise shift of the sonic line relative to the blunt geometry as Mach numbers increased.
